Arithmetic and Geometric Sequences: Patterns in Mathematics

📅 2025-05-04 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

Sequences are ordered lists of numbers following a pattern — and they appear throughout mathematics, science, and nature. The spiral of a nautilus shell follows a geometric sequence. The distance covered each second by a falling object follows an arithmetic sequence (ignoring air resistance). Financial calculations like loan payments and compound interest use geometric sequences. Understanding sequences and series is essential for calculus (where series become a powerful tool for approximating functions), discrete mathematics, and computer science algorithms. The two most fundamental types are arithmetic sequences (constant difference) and geometric sequences (constant ratio).

Arithmetic Sequences

An arithmetic sequence has a constant common difference d between consecutive terms. General form: a, a+d, a+2d, a+3d, ... The nth term: aₙ = a₁ + (n−1)d. Sum of first n terms (arithmetic series): Sₙ = n/2 × (a₁ + aₙ) = n/2 × (2a₁ + (n−1)d). Example: 3, 7, 11, 15, 19, ... has a₁=3 and d=4. The 20th term: a₂₀ = 3 + 19×4 = 79. Sum of first 20 terms: S₂₀ = 20/2 × (3+79) = 10×82 = 820. Fun fact: the sum 1+2+3+...+100 is an arithmetic series. Gauss (at age 10) famously paired numbers: (1+100)+(2+99)+...+(50+51) = 50×101 = 5050.

Key Point: Check if |r| < 1 before attempting an infinite geometric series — otherwise it diverges.

Geometric Sequences

A geometric sequence has a constant common ratio r between consecutive terms. General form: a, ar, ar², ar³, ... The nth term: aₙ = a₁ × r^(n−1). Sum of first n terms: Sₙ = a₁(1−rⁿ)/(1−r) for r≠1. Example: 2, 6, 18, 54, ... has a₁=2 and r=3. The 8th term: a₈ = 2×3⁷ = 2×2187 = 4374. S₅ = 2(1−3⁵)/(1−3) = 2(−242)/(−2) = 242. Infinite geometric series: if |r|<1, the sum converges to S∞ = a₁/(1−r). Example: 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ... = (1/2)/(1−1/2) = 1. This elegant result shows that infinitely many positive numbers can sum to a finite value.

Identifying and Applying Sequences

To identify the type: compute differences (a₂−a₁, a₃−a₂, ...) — if constant, arithmetic. Compute ratios (a₂/a₁, a₃/a₂, ...) — if constant, geometric. Applications: compound interest A = P(1+r)ⁿ is geometric with ratio (1+r). Depreciation and population growth use geometric models. Fibonacci sequence 1,1,2,3,5,8,13... (each term is the sum of the two before) appears in nature. Arithmetic sequences model simple interest, equally-spaced events, and linear relationships. When solving sequence problems, always clearly identify a₁, d or r, and which term n you need.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1
An arithmetic sequence has a₁ = 5 and a₁₀ = 32. Find d and the sum of the first 10 terms.
a₁₀ = a₁ + 9d → 32 = 5 + 9d → d = 3 S₁₀ = 10/2 × (5+32) = 5 × 37 = 185
Exercise 2
A ball is dropped from 16 m. Each bounce reaches 3/4 of the previous height. What total distance does it travel?
First drop: 16m. Then bounces: 12, 9, 6.75, ... Total = 16 + 2×(12+9+6.75+...) = 16 + 2 × 12/(1−0.75) = 16 + 2×48 = 16 + 96 = 112 meters
💡 Study Tip: Check if |r| < 1 before attempting an infinite geometric series — otherwise it diverges.

Conclusion

Sequences and series reveal pattern and structure hiding in numbers. Arithmetic sequences grow steadily; geometric sequences grow exponentially. Mastering their formulas lets you solve problems in finance, physics, and biology with elegant efficiency. The fact that an infinite geometric series can converge to a finite sum is a beautiful and surprising result that opens the door to calculus and the study of infinite processes. Build your intuition by generating sequences by hand, then practicing the formulas on varied problems.
