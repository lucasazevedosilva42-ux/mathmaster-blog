Logarithms and Exponential Functions: A Step-by-Step Guide

📅 2025-05-04 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

Logarithms were invented in the 17th century to simplify multiplication of large numbers — and they remain one of the most powerful tools in mathematics today. A logarithm answers the question: to what power must we raise the base to get this number? If 2³ = 8, then log₂(8) = 3. Logarithms are the inverse of exponential functions, and together they model growth and decay in biology, finance, chemistry, and physics. The decibel scale for sound, the Richter scale for earthquakes, and pH in chemistry are all logarithmic. Mastering logs means understanding the language of exponential change.

Logarithm Rules You Must Know

The four essential log rules: Product Rule: log(ab) = log a + log b. Quotient Rule: log(a/b) = log a − log b. Power Rule: log(aⁿ) = n·log a. Change of Base: log_b(x) = ln(x)/ln(b). Examples: log₂(32) = log₂(2⁵) = 5. log(100) = log(10²) = 2. ln(e³) = 3. To solve 2ˣ = 50: take log of both sides → x·log 2 = log 50 → x = log(50)/log(2) = 1.699/0.301 ≈ 5.64. Common bases: log = log₁₀ (common log), ln = logₑ (natural log, base e ≈ 2.718). The natural log is used in calculus and science because its derivative is beautifully simple.

Key Point: The change of base formula log_b(x) = ln(x)/ln(b) works on any calculator.

Solving Exponential and Logarithmic Equations

For exponential equations, take the log of both sides. For log equations, exponentiate both sides. Example 1 (exponential): 3^(x+1) = 81 → 3^(x+1) = 3⁴ → x+1 = 4 → x = 3. Example 2: 5^x = 200 → x = log(200)/log(5) = 2.301/0.699 ≈ 3.29. Example 3 (logarithmic): log₂(x+3) = 5 → x+3 = 2⁵ = 32 → x = 29. Example 4: ln(2x) = 4 → 2x = e⁴ ≈ 54.6 → x ≈ 27.3. Always check domain restrictions: you cannot take the log of zero or a negative number. If your solution makes the argument negative, discard it.

Exponential Growth, Decay, and Real Applications

The compound interest formula A = P·eʳᵗ uses the natural log to find time: t = ln(A/P)/r. Population growth: N(t) = N₀·eᵏᵗ. Radioactive decay: N(t) = N₀·e^(−λt). Half-life formula: t½ = ln(2)/λ. The pH scale: pH = −log[H⁺]. Earthquake magnitude: M = log(I/I₀). These real-world applications show why logs are indispensable. When a quantity grows or decays at a rate proportional to itself, the answer always involves e and the natural log.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1
Solve for x: log₃(x) + log₃(x−2) = 1
Combine using product rule: log₃(x(x−2)) = 1 x(x−2) = 3¹ = 3 x²−2x−3 = 0 (x−3)(x+1) = 0 x = 3 or x = −1 Discard x = −1 (log of negative undefined) → x = 3
Exercise 2
A bacteria population doubles every 3 hours. Starting from 500, when will it reach 16,000?
16000 = 500·2^(t/3) 32 = 2^(t/3) 2⁵ = 2^(t/3) t/3 = 5 → t = 15 hours

Conclusion

Logarithms reward patience. The rules take time to internalize, but once they click, you will see them everywhere. Practice applying each rule individually before combining them. Always check domain restrictions when solving log equations. The connection between exponential growth and logarithms is one of the deepest and most beautiful relationships in mathematics — master it and you will have a powerful tool for science, finance, and beyond.
