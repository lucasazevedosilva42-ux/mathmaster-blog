Integrals and Antiderivatives: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

📅 2026-05-07 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is a cornerstone of mathematics, connecting derivatives and integrals. It's crucial for problem-solving in physics, engineering, and economics. In this post, we'll explore the theorem, its components, and practical applications, making calculus more accessible and fun to learn. By understanding integrals and antiderivatives, you'll unlock the doors to advanced mathematical concepts and real-world problem-solving.

Introduction to Antiderivatives

An antiderivative of a function f(x) is a function F(x) such that F'(x) = f(x). In other words, it's a function that, when differentiated, returns the original function. For example, the antiderivative of x^2 is (1/3)x^3 + C, where C is the constant of integration. To find antiderivatives, we can use various techniques such as substitution, integration by parts, and integration by partial fractions. These techniques will be essential in solving a wide range of problems in calculus.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus states that differentiation and integration are inverse processes. It consists of two main parts: the first part states that the derivative of an integral is equal to the original function, while the second part states that the integral of a derivative is equal to the original function plus a constant. Mathematically, this can be expressed as ∫[a, b] f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a), where F(x) is the antiderivative of f(x). This theorem provides a powerful tool for evaluating definite integrals and solving a wide range of problems in calculus.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1
Find the antiderivative of the function f(x) = 2x + 1.
To find the antiderivative, we'll integrate the function with respect to x. Step 1: Integrate 2x, which gives x^2. Step 2: Integrate 1, which gives x. Step 3: Combine the results, giving the antiderivative as x^2 + x + C, where C is the constant of integration.
Exercise 2
Evaluate the definite integral ∫[0, 2] x^2 dx.
To evaluate the definite integral, we'll use the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. Step 1: Find the antiderivative of x^2, which is (1/3)x^3. Step 2: Evaluate the antiderivative at the upper limit of integration, giving (1/3)(2)^3 = 8/3. Step 3: Evaluate the antiderivative at the lower limit of integration, giving (1/3)(0)^3 = 0. Step 4: Subtract the results, giving the final answer as 8/3 - 0 = 8/3.
