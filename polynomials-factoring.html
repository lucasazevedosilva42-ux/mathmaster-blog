Polynomials and Factoring: Complete Algebra Tutorial

📅 2025-05-04 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

Polynomials are algebraic expressions built from variables and constants using addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents. They form the foundation of algebra and appear throughout calculus, engineering, and computer science. The word comes from Greek: poly (many) + nomial (term). From simple linear expressions to complex degree-five equations, polynomials model curves, approximate functions, and encode information. Factoring — the process of writing a polynomial as a product of simpler pieces — is one of the most important skills in algebra. It enables solving equations, simplifying expressions, and understanding the structure of mathematical functions.

Types and Terminology

A polynomial in x has the form aₙxⁿ + aₙ₋₁xⁿ⁻¹ + ... + a₁x + a₀. The degree is the highest power. Degree 0: constant (e.g., 7). Degree 1: linear (3x+2). Degree 2: quadratic (x²−5x+6). Degree 3: cubic (2x³+x²−x+4). The leading coefficient is the coefficient of the highest-degree term. The constant term is a₀. To add/subtract polynomials, combine like terms (same variable and exponent). To multiply, use distribution: (x+2)(x+3) = x²+3x+2x+6 = x²+5x+6. The FOIL method (First, Outer, Inner, Last) is a memory aid for multiplying two binomials.

Key Point: Always check for GCF first before applying other factoring techniques.

Factoring Techniques

Always start by finding the Greatest Common Factor (GCF). 6x³+9x² → GCF is 3x² → 3x²(2x+3). Difference of squares: a²−b² = (a+b)(a−b). Example: x²−25 = (x+5)(x−5). Perfect square trinomials: a²+2ab+b² = (a+b)². Sum/difference of cubes: a³+b³=(a+b)(a²−ab+b²). Factoring ax²+bx+c: find two numbers that multiply to ac and add to b. For x²+5x+6: find two numbers → product=6, sum=5 → 2 and 3 → (x+2)(x+3). For 2x²+7x+3: ac=6, need product 6 sum 7 → 1 and 6 → 2x²+x+6x+3 → x(2x+1)+3(2x+1) → (x+3)(2x+1).

Polynomial Division and the Remainder Theorem

Polynomial long division works like numerical long division. Divide x³−2x²+x−3 by (x−2): quotient is x²+1 remainder −1. Synthetic division is a faster method when dividing by (x−c). The Remainder Theorem: when polynomial P(x) is divided by (x−c), the remainder is P(c). The Factor Theorem: (x−c) is a factor of P(x) if and only if P(c)=0. So to check if (x−3) is a factor of x³−6x²+11x−6, evaluate P(3)=27−54+33−6=0 ✓, confirming it is a factor. This leads to finding all roots of polynomials systematically using the Rational Root Theorem.

Conclusion

Polynomials and factoring are the grammar of algebra. Every technique — GCF, difference of squares, trinomial factoring, grouping — expands your ability to simplify and solve increasingly complex expressions. The Factor Theorem and Remainder Theorem connect factoring to function evaluation, bridging algebra and calculus. Build speed through practice, and learn to recognize which technique to apply first. The satisfaction of factoring a complex polynomial is one of the genuine pleasures of advanced algebra.
