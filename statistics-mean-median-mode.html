Statistics Basics: Mean, Median, Mode and Standard Deviation

📅 2025-05-04 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

Statistics is the science of making sense of data — and in the age of information, it has never been more important. Whether you are analyzing test scores, comparing salaries, or understanding poll results, statistical thinking helps you see the signal in the noise. The three measures of central tendency — mean, median, and mode — describe where data tends to cluster. Measures of spread — range, variance, and standard deviation — describe how scattered the data is. Together, these tools give you a complete picture of any dataset. Statistics is not just for scientists: it is the foundation of data-driven decision making in business, medicine, sports, and everyday life.

Mean, Median, and Mode

Given the dataset: 4, 7, 7, 9, 12, 15, 15, 15, 20. Mean (average): add all values and divide by n. Sum = 104, n = 9, mean = 104/9 ≈ 11.56. Median (middle value): arrange in order and find the center. With 9 values, median = 5th value = 12. Mode (most frequent): 15 appears 3 times, so mode = 15. When to use each: mean works best for symmetric data without outliers. Median is better when outliers are present (e.g., income data where a few billionaires skew the mean). Mode is used for categorical data or when you need the most common value. A dataset can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes.

Key Point: Use the median instead of the mean when your data has extreme outliers.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures the average squared deviation from the mean. Formula: σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N (population) or s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n−1) (sample). Standard deviation (σ or s) is the square root of variance — it has the same units as the original data. Example with dataset 2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9: mean = 5. Deviations: −3, −1, −1, −1, 0, 0, 2, 4. Squared deviations: 9, 1, 1, 1, 0, 0, 4, 16. Sum = 32. Variance = 32/8 = 4. Std deviation = √4 = 2. Interpretation: about 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean (the 68-95-99.7 rule for normal distributions).

Interpreting Data: Box Plots and Z-Scores

A box plot (box-and-whisker) shows the five-number summary: minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum. The interquartile range (IQR = Q3 − Q1) measures spread of the middle 50%. Outliers are typically defined as values below Q1 − 1.5×IQR or above Q3 + 1.5×IQR. Z-scores standardize data: z = (x − μ) / σ. A z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. z = 2 means the value is 2 standard deviations above average. Z-scores allow comparison across different datasets with different scales.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1
Find the mean, median, and mode of: 3, 5, 5, 7, 8, 9, 9, 9, 11
Mean: (3+5+5+7+8+9+9+9+11)/9 = 66/9 ≈ 7.33 Median: 9 values, middle (5th) = 8 Mode: 9 appears 3 times → mode = 9
Exercise 2
A test has mean 72 and std deviation 8. What is the z-score of a student who scored 88?
z = (88 − 72) / 8 = 16/8 = 2.0 This student scored 2 standard deviations above the mean, which is better than approximately 97.7% of students.
💡 Study Tip: Use the median instead of the mean when your data has extreme outliers.

Conclusion

Statistics gives you the power to understand and communicate with data. The mean, median, and mode each tell a different story about a dataset — knowing when to use each one is crucial. Standard deviation quantifies uncertainty and variability. As you study more advanced statistics, you will build on these fundamentals in regression, probability, and hypothesis testing. Start by practicing on real datasets: your grades, sports statistics, or weather data. Statistics is most powerful when applied to things you genuinely care about.
