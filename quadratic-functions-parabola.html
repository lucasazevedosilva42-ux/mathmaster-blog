Quadratic Functions and Parabolas: Everything You Need to Know

📅 2025-05-04 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

Quadratic functions are the next level after linear equations, and they describe some of the most beautiful curves in mathematics — parabolas. You see parabolas everywhere: the arc of a basketball, the cables of a suspension bridge, the path of a thrown ball under gravity. A quadratic function has the form f(x) = ax² + bx + c, where a ≠ 0. The graph is always a parabola: U-shaped if a > 0 (opens upward) or ∩-shaped if a < 0 (opens downward). Understanding quadratics means you can find maximum heights, optimal values, and intersection points — skills that appear in physics, economics, and engineering every day.

Key Features of a Parabola

Every parabola has three essential features. The vertex is the highest or lowest point, found at x = −b/(2a) and y = f(−b/(2a)). The axis of symmetry is the vertical line x = −b/(2a) that splits the parabola into two mirror halves. The roots (or zeros) are where the parabola crosses the x-axis, found by setting f(x) = 0. For f(x) = x² − 4x + 3: vertex at x = 2, y = −1, so vertex is (2, −1). Axis of symmetry: x = 2. Roots: factor to (x−1)(x−3) = 0, so x = 1 and x = 3. The parabola opens upward (a = 1 > 0) and crosses the x-axis at (1, 0) and (3, 0).

Key Point: Always compute the discriminant first — it tells you how many real solutions exist.

Solving Quadratic Equations: Three Methods

Method 1 — Factoring: x² + 5x + 6 = 0 → (x+2)(x+3) = 0 → x = −2 or x = −3. Fast when the equation factors nicely. Method 2 — Completing the Square: rewrite ax² + bx + c = 0 in the form (x+h)² = k, then take square roots. Method 3 — Quadratic Formula: x = [−b ± √(b²−4ac)] / (2a). Works for every quadratic, even when factoring is impossible. The discriminant b²−4ac tells you the nature of the roots: positive → two real roots, zero → one repeated root, negative → no real roots (complex). For 2x²−3x−2=0: x = [3 ± √(9+16)] / 4 = [3 ± 5] / 4, giving x = 2 or x = −0.5.

Vertex Form and Applications

Vertex form f(x) = a(x−h)² + k makes reading the vertex (h, k) instant. Converting from standard form: complete the square. Quadratics model projectile motion: h(t) = −16t² + v₀t + h₀ (in feet). The maximum height occurs at t = v₀/32. In economics, profit functions are often quadratic — the vertex gives the maximum profit. Practice converting between standard and vertex forms, and always sketch a rough graph: knowing whether the parabola opens up or down immediately tells you whether the vertex is a minimum or maximum.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1
Find the vertex and roots of f(x) = x² − 6x + 5
Vertex: x = −(−6)/(2·1) = 3, y = 9−18+5 = −4 → vertex (3, −4) Roots: x²−6x+5 = (x−1)(x−5) = 0 → x = 1 and x = 5 Parabola opens upward, vertex is minimum point.
Exercise 2
A ball is thrown upward with h(t) = −5t² + 20t + 2. What is the maximum height?
Maximum at t = −20/(2·(−5)) = 2 seconds Max height: h(2) = −5(4) + 20(2) + 2 = −20 + 40 + 2 = 22 meters

Conclusion

Quadratic functions are one of the most powerful tools in mathematics. From projectile motion to profit optimization, parabolas model the world around us. Master all three solving methods — factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula — so you can choose the fastest approach for each problem. Practice graphing: the ability to sketch a parabola quickly from its equation is a skill that will serve you in every math course ahead.
