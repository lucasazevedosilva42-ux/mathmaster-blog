Combinatorics: Permutations and Combinations Explained

📅 2026-05-08 ⏱️ 8 min read 📐 MathMaster Blog

Combinatorics, the study of counting and arranging objects in various ways, is a fundamental concept in mathematics with numerous real-world applications. From calculating the number of possible outcomes in a game to determining the probability of certain events, combinatorics plays a vital role in fields such as computer science, engineering, and statistics. In this blog post, we will delve into the world of combinatorics, exploring the concepts of permutations and combinations, and providing examples and exercises to help you master these essential skills.

Permutations

A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order. For example, if we have three letters: A, B, and C, there are six possible permutations: ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, and CBA. To calculate the number of permutations, we use the formula n!, where n is the number of objects. In this case, 3! = 3 * 2 * 1 = 6. We can also calculate permutations with repetition, where objects can be repeated. For instance, if we have two letters: A and B, and we want to form a three-letter word, there are 2^3 = 8 possible permutations: AAA, AAB, ABA, ABB, BAA, BAB, BBA, and BBB.

Combinations

A combination is a selection of objects without regard to order. For example, if we have three letters: A, B, and C, and we want to choose two letters, there are three possible combinations: AB, AC, and BC. To calculate the number of combinations, we use the formula nCr = n! / (r!(n-r)!), where n is the total number of objects and r is the number of objects chosen. In this case, 3C2 = 3! / (2!(3-2)!) = 3. We can also calculate combinations with repetition, where objects can be repeated. For instance, if we have two letters: A and B, and we want to choose three letters, there are four possible combinations: AAA, AAB, ABB, and BBB.

Practice and Real-World Applications

Combinatorics has numerous real-world applications, including calculating the number of possible outcomes in a game, determining the probability of certain events, and optimizing systems. To practice combinatorics, try solving problems involving permutations and combinations, and apply these concepts to real-world scenarios.

Practice Exercises

Exercise 1
If a pizza parlor offers 5 different toppings, how many different pizza combinations can be made with 3 toppings?
Step 1: Identify the total number of toppings (n) as 5 and the number of toppings to choose (r) as 3. Step 2: Calculate the number of combinations using the formula nCr = n! / (r!(n-r)!). Step 3: Plug in the values: 5C3 = 5! / (3!(5-3)!) = 10. Step 4: Therefore, there are 10 different pizza combinations that can be made with 3 toppings.
Exercise 2
If a committee of 5 members is to be formed from a group of 10 people, how many different committees can be formed?
Step 1: Identify the total number of people (n) as 10 and the number of people to choose (r) as 5. Step 2: Calculate the number of combinations using the formula nCr = n! / (r!(n-r)!). Step 3: Plug in the values: 10C5 = 10! / (5!(10-5)!) = 252. Step 4: Therefore, there are 252 different committees that can be formed.
